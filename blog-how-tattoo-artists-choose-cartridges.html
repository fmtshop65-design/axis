How Professional Tattoo Artists Choose the Right Cartridge

The 4-step decision framework that experienced artists use to select the right needle configuration, size, and cartridge brand for every session.

← Back to Blog

The Decision Framework Professional Artists Use

Choosing a cartridge is not guesswork — it is a deliberate decision based on the style, the area of the body, the client's skin type, and the machine being used. Here is how experienced artists think through the choice.

Step 1: Identify the Technique

  • Fine-line linework: 1RL–5RL. The smaller the number, the finer the line. Use 1–3RL for single-needle micro-detail, 5–7RL for standard portrait outlines.
  • Bold traditional outlines: 7RL–18RL. Larger liners deliver more ink per pass for thick, saturated black lines.
  • Greywash realism: 9RS–14RS Round Shader or Curved Magnum — wide ink spread with soft edges.
  • Colour saturation: 11M1–23M1 Magnum. Covers large areas quickly with full colour opacity.
  • Geometric blackwork: 7FS–14FS Flat Shader for hard edges and solid fills.

Step 2: Consider Skin Type

Skin type significantly affects needle choice:

  • Thin, sensitive skin: Use smaller needle counts. A 7RS instead of a 14RS. More frequent light passes rather than fewer heavy ones.
  • Thicker skin (hands, feet, elbows): Larger configurations with a steeper angle to ensure full pigment deposit.
  • Older skin: Avoid very small liners — skin loses elasticity and fine lines can bleed more easily. Use moderate-size liners at a slower pace.

Step 3: Match the Machine

Machine stroke length affects which cartridges perform best:

  • Short stroke (2.5–3.5mm): Better for liners and detail — faster movement, precise control.
  • Long stroke (3.5–4.5mm+): Better for shaders, magnums, and colour — more ink pushed per hit.

AXIS cartridges are membrane-equipped, meaning they maintain consistent ink flow regardless of stroke length — a key advantage when switching machines mid-session.

Step 4: Factor In Session Length

Needle cartridges degrade over time. Tips become micro-dulled after 2–3 hours of continuous use. For large-scale pieces, professional artists plan cartridge changes:

  • Start the session: Fresh cartridge for the most detail-critical work (faces, lettering).
  • Mid-session: New cartridge for large colour fills — a slightly dulled needle tears skin during saturation passes.
  • End of session: A third cartridge if doing extensive background or cover-up work.
